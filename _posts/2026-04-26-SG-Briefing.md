## THE GROUND PICTURE

The material reality of living in Singapore is currently defined by a sharp divergence between high-level economic growth and the daily cost of survival. While the **Straits Times Index** and key exports show strength driven by global demand for electronics, the average professional is facing a "two-speed" economy. War in the Middle East has moved from a distant headline to a direct pressure on the local cost stack, specifically through rising petrol prices and utility bills.

At the same time, the workplace is undergoing a structural shift that is more aggressive than previous cycles of automation. The government is no longer just talking about artificial intelligence; it is actively embedding it into the education system and professional standards. For the working professional, this means job security is becoming tied to the ability to operate alongside AI "agents" rather than just using software. The safety net is being adjusted through targeted cash payouts and rebates, but the underlying costs of housing and transport remain at historic highs.

## ECONOMY AND COST OF LIVING

Inflation in Singapore is accelerating, reaching 1.8% in March, with transport and retail costs leading the surge. The **Iran War** and the resulting blockade of the **Strait of Hormuz** have created a persistent energy crisis that filters down to every household. **DBS** and **POSB** have committed S$10 million in cashback redemptions for hawker meals and groceries to help residents manage these pressures. The government has also introduced a S$1 billion support package, which includes higher cash payouts and **CDC Vouchers** to offset the rising cost of living.

The housing market shows a rare split in performance. Public housing resale prices recorded their first dip in nearly seven years during the first quarter of 2026, providing slight relief for some buyers. However, private home prices continue to rise, driven by strong demand for new launches in the suburbs. For those with existing mortgages, the environment is challenging; some households are now struggling with monthly payments as high as S$12,000. **OCBC** has announced a reduction in the maximum interest rate for its flagship savings account, signaling that the period of high returns on cash deposits may be ending.

Transport costs remain a significant burden on the middle-class budget. The **Certificate of Entitlement (COE)** for Category A cars has surpassed S$123,000, making car ownership increasingly inaccessible for many professionals. While the government is providing targeted support for platform workers and essential bus services, the broader cost of moving people and goods across the island is not easing. The core dynamic is a cost stack that is being pushed upward by global energy shocks while local savings and subsidies struggle to keep pace.

## EMPLOYMENT AND INDUSTRY

The job market is currently characterized by high anxiety and rapid redesign. Over 4,700 workers were retrenched in a single two-week period in early 2026, reflecting a weakening outlook in sectors sensitive to global trade. In the technology sector, the focus has shifted from general hiring to specialized AI roles. Firms are increasingly using the **Jobs Transformation Map** framework to identify roles in accounting, finance, and aviation that require immediate reskilling. High-impact roles like audit associates and passenger service agents are facing significant changes as **Agentic AI** begins to handle routine documentation and resource allocation.

Competition for roles is being reshaped by the **COMPASS** salary rules, which are forcing firms to rethink their foreign hiring strategies. This is happening alongside a rise in "quiet firing" and workplace gaslighting, as reported by employees navigating leaner corporate structures. In the semiconductor industry, major players like **UMC** are reporting talent strain at their Singapore fabrication plants, even as they benefit from an AI-linked export surge. The government is attempting to address these gaps by uplifting skilled trades, such as electricians, through structured career ladders.

For IT professionals, Singapore is emerging as a "neutral ground" for AI firms navigating the rivalry between the US and China. This has led to an influx of investment from firms seeking to avoid unpredictable US visa processes or Chinese regulatory crackdowns. However, this also means local professionals are competing in a globalized talent pool where skills in prompt design and AI ethics are becoming mandatory. The core dynamic is a widening gap between those whose roles are augmented by AI and those whose roles are being displaced by it.

## GOVERNMENT DIRECTION

The government is steering the nation toward a "Smart Nation 2.0" vision, with a heavy emphasis on AI integration and national resilience. Significant funding is being directed through the **Financial Sector Technology and Innovation Scheme (FSTI 3.0)** and the **AI Sandbox for SMEs** to ensure local businesses do not fall behind. In schools, the **Ministry of Education** is introducing AI to Primary 4 pupils, despite some parental concerns about age-appropriateness. This move signals a long-term commitment to making AI literacy a foundational skill for the future workforce.

Beyond technology, policy attention is focused on the "silver tsunami" of an ageing population. The government is encouraging the development of assisted-living facilities and has launched the **Smart Diabetes Management Family Programme** to tackle long-term healthcare costs. There is also a clear move toward tightening regulations in the "low-altitude economy," with stricter rules for drone usage and personal mobility devices. To maintain social cohesion during global conflicts, the government is emphasizing community mediation and has reopened the **Malay Heritage Centre** to reinforce a shared sense of home.

National security definitions are expanding to include domestic social stability and energy resilience. The **Ministry of Home Affairs** and the **National Environment Agency** are enhancing nuclear safety capabilities through a five-year framework with the **International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA)**. Additionally, the **National Service** medical classification system is being refreshed to better match personnel to roles in a more technologically complex defense environment. The core direction is the aggressive modernization of both the economy and social services to withstand external shocks.

## REGIONAL POSITION

Singapore’s regional position is being tested by both competition and new opportunities for integration. The **Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ)** and the **RTS Link** project are central to a strategy of building a "Southern Malaysia supply chain" that supports advanced manufacturing. This allows Singapore to remain the high-value hub while leveraging Malaysia’s lower land and labor costs. However, this relationship is complicated by domestic pressures in Malaysia, where the government is facing calls for deeper anti-corruption reforms and is reviewing its own fuel subsidy policies.

In the wider region, Thailand is proposing a S$40 billion land bridge to bypass the **Strait of Malacca**, which could eventually challenge Singapore’s maritime dominance. Indonesia is also making aggressive moves in the electric vehicle (EV) sector, utilizing its nickel production to power a global battery boom. While Indonesia has reiterated that it will not impose transit fees in the **Strait of Malacca**, the rumor of such a move highlights the fragility of regional trade agreements. Singapore is responding by investing over S$100 million in maritime research to maintain its lead in port efficiency.

Capital is currently flowing into Singapore from wealthy families in the Middle East and North Asia who view the city-state as a safe haven amid global instability. This influx of private wealth is being courted to fund public growth, but it also contributes to the high cost of luxury property and services. Regional airlines are seeing a surge in passengers as travelers reroute through Southeast Asia to avoid the conflict zones in the Gulf. The core dynamic is Singapore positioning itself as the indispensable, stable node in an increasingly volatile and competitive Southeast Asian landscape.

## GLOBAL FORCES LANDING LOCALLY

The **Iran War** is the primary global force affecting Singapore’s material conditions today. The US-led blockade of Iranian ports and the resulting shipping crisis in the **Strait of Hormuz** have pushed global energy prices to multi-year highs. This has a direct impact on Singapore’s electricity prices and food security, as regional farmers in Thailand and Vietnam face higher costs for fuel and fertilizer. The **United Nations** has warned that a prolonged closure of these shipping lanes could lead to a humanitarian and economic catastrophe that Singapore cannot entirely subsidize its way out of.

The ongoing tech war between the US and China is also landing in Singapore’s data centers and boardrooms. The US State Department has issued warnings regarding AI thefts by Chinese firms like **DeepSeek**, while **GIC** and **Temasek** continue to make massive investments in US-based AI startups like **Anthropic**. This forces Singapore to maintain a delicate balance, acting as a bridge for capital and technology while complying with tightening global sanctions and export controls. The global shift toward "resource nationalism" is also visible as Singapore secures legally binding agreements with partners like Australia to ensure the continued flow of LNG and diesel.
